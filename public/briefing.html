Executive summary & introduction

A concise overview of the project, its goal, how participation works, and practical next steps in Canada — written for public servants, elected officials, and community leaders.

“Privacy is not a feature. It is a birthright.”

Why this matters now

Across the world, public and private power over human life increasingly runs through data, platforms, and algorithms. Profiling, automated decisions, and surveillance at scale often operate without clear explanation, meaningful consent, or equal recourse. Existing laws help — yet they remain fragmented, slow to adapt to artificial intelligence, and uneven across borders.

SherpaCarta answers a simple civic need: a shared, living statement of digital human rights that people can read, sign, improve, and carry into law-making — with humility, transparency, and respect for every language and community.

What SherpaCarta is

It is

  • A living charter of 114 articles on privacy, data sovereignty, expression, and algorithmic rights
  • A moral and political declaration — successor in spirit to the 1215 Magna Carta and the 2011 Icelandic crowdsourced constitution
  • CC0 public domain — free to copy, translate, and adapt
  • Funded only by voluntary Bitcoin donations; open source; zero tracking
  • Open to everyone: any language, any age to join the conversation; inclusive by design

It is not (yet)

  • Binding legislation or a treaty on its own
  • A partisan project or corporate compliance product
  • A surveillance or data-harvesting platform — we do not build dossiers of signers
  • A claim that technology alone replaces democratic process

Its power grows through adoption: citizens, organizations, and eventually statutes that echo its principles.

The goal

To establish a clear, global, people-owned reference for digital rights — and to help turn that moral force into better law and practice, starting where partners are ready. Rights may only expand, never contract (Art. 114).

Four pillars (at a glance)

01
Privacy first — communications, data, and identity protected by default
02
Universal access — connectivity treated as essential infrastructure
03
Freedom of expression — speech protected from arbitrary state or platform power
04
Data sovereignty — people own and control their personal data

Also central: algorithmic transparency and non-discrimination (Arts. 61–62), right to erasure (Art. 47), and limits on surveillance capitalism (Art. 13).

How it works

Step 1
Read the charter 114 articles online, full text, open API. Search, share, and study — no account required.
Step 2
Sign & support Campaign commitments are local-first and privacy-preserving. Optional public proofs (e.g. Nostr, cryptographic receipts). Paper and official parliamentary petitions use government rules.
Step 3
Shape law carefully Model language, petitions, and briefings help legislators adopt principles — not overnight, but with clear public mandate and transparent process.

Honesty about signatures

We carefully separate campaign support (movement-building, cryptographic receipts, public discourse) from official parliamentary instruments (House of Commons e-petition or original paper petitions presented by a Member). That honesty protects the integrity of both democratic procedure and the project.

Canada — a practical beachhead

SherpaCarta is global. Canada is a natural first jurisdiction: strong privacy culture, federal–provincial law-making, and room to modernize frameworks such as PIPEDA for the age of AI and platforms. British Columbia is often our organizing focus — not because other provinces matter less, but because every movement needs a place to begin. A signature from any province or territory on a federal paper petition already counts as a Canadian federal signature.

Respectful ask of elected friends: (1) Review the charter and this briefing; (2) Consider authorizing or presenting a federal petition when the text is ready; (3) Use SherpaCarta as model language when modernizing digital privacy and algorithmic accountability — without requiring agreement on every article at once.

Closing note

We do not claim perfection. We claim a shared starting point: that human dignity online deserves the same seriousness once given to liberty under parchment law. We welcome critique, translation, amendment (rights only expand), and partnership from every community and political tradition that defends the person against arbitrary power.
